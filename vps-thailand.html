Hypervisor-like technologies are fast becoming the de facto standard for virtualization. Virtualization is becoming an integral part of most enterprise solutions due to its flexibility, cost effectiveness, and the ability to run various virtual environments in a single management fabric. A number of open-source technologies are also increasing in popularity. Virtualization is especially popular with businesses that have their own data center or servers, but it is useful for other organizations as well. Both enterprise and lower-end desktop and server VDI are making use of some form of virtualization technology.


KVM is an open-source, cross-platform virtualization solution for both the Linux kernel and the Windows operating system. KVM works very similarly to VMware. It divides the virtualization technologies into two distinct categories: Server and Client. KVM works best with the Linux kernel, but can be adapted to any OS.


In terms of performance, KVM is clearly much faster than its VMware competitor. However, this advantage is offset by the greater complexity of the underlying software and greater reliance on the Linux operating system for root access. In terms of usability, both systems are extremely easy to use. In terms of total cost of ownership (TCO), both programs are fairly close to each other, though KVM is slightly more affordable. Overall, both supervisors appear to be the easiest way to visualize an organization's servers.


In many ways, both these technologies are incredibly similar. They both have the ability to create, restore, manage, and destroy virtual machines, replicating the functionality of physical machines. They both also both use networking to allow a VDI to access other VMs. However, there are several key differences between the two.
